Untitled Negotiation

Setup

Start here. Define who you're negotiating with, what both sides want, what's driving each party to the table, and any hard constraints. The timeline check at the bottom will tell you if your plan is realistic once you've built it out.

Their Side
Our Objectives
What do you want to walk away with? These are specific, measurable outcomes: a price point, a contract term, a volume commitment. Not why you're negotiating -- that's a driver.
Our Drivers
Why are you at the table at all? A contract expiring in 90 days is a driver. A competitor entering your market is a driver. Drivers explain the urgency and motivation behind the negotiation, for both sides.
Our Restrictions
Hard limits you can't or won't cross. These aren't negotiating positions, they're genuine constraints: board mandates, regulatory requirements, budget ceilings, policy red lines.
Their Objectives
Your best assessment of what they want from this negotiation. You won't know everything, but write down what you can infer from past behavior, public statements, or market position.
Their Drivers
What's pushing them to negotiate? Are they under competitive pressure? Do they have a fiscal year deadline? Understanding their drivers helps you understand their flexibility.
Their Restrictions
What constraints are they operating under? Regulatory limits, internal policies, public commitments they can't walk back.

Variables

List every variable you could trade in this negotiation. A variable is anything that has value to either side and could be adjusted as part of a deal: distribution commitments, contract length, payment terms, service levels, exclusivity, volume guarantees, marketing support, review periods, and price. Start broad. Don't filter yet. The next step will help you sort them.

Ranking

For each variable, rate its importance and difficulty for each side. A strong Take is something important to you that isn't difficult for them to concede. A strong Give is the opposite: important to them, not difficult for you. When something is important AND difficult for both sides, it becomes a Both variable -- expect this to be the hardest part of the negotiation.

Proposals

Build your proposals as conditional packages: 'If you do X, then we'll do Y.' Select a variable on each side and enter the specific position you're proposing. Each proposal should pair at least one Take with at least one Give. Keep proposals simple -- two to three conditions is usually enough for the other side to evaluate.

Meetings

Plan your meetings, calls, and emails. Assign proposals from the previous step to specific touchpoints. The timeline at the top shows whether your plan is realistic given your deadline. Meetings without proposals assigned aren't wasted; they might be for information gathering, relationship building, or responding to their moves.

Tracker

Track what was actually proposed and countered, round by round. Don't repeat positions that haven't changed; leave those cells empty and the tool will carry forward the last known position visually. The Status column shows you where alignment exists, where movement has stalled, and which variables haven't been raised yet. If everything except price is aligned, that's a signal to address it directly rather than circling around it.
